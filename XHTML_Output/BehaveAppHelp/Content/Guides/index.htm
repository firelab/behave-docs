Surface Module

The Surface module is the foundation of Behave. The Surface module is applicable in non-forested systems such as grasslands or shrublands or forests with a sparse overstory. The Surface module does not consider torching or active crown fire in forested systems, even if there is potential for torching or active crown fire.

There are three options for the direction of spread including:

  • Heading – outputs are calculated for a heading fire in the direction of maximum spread.

  • Heading, Backing, Flanking – outputs are calculated for a heading fire in the direction of maximum spread, backing fire that is 180 degrees from a heading fire, and flanking fire that is 90 degrees from a heading fire.

  • Direction of Interest – custom input in degrees from upslope.

Two spotting models are only available in the Surface module:

  • Maximum spotting distance from a wind-driven grass fire

  • Maximum spotting distance from a pile

Assumptions and Limitations of the Surface Spread Model

  1. Fire is predicted at the flaming front.

  2. The fire is free burning.

    1. Does not account for suppression actions

    2. The ignition origin is not influencing the fire.

  3. Fine fuels (<1 inch in diameter) are more important to the fire’s spread than larger fuels.

  4. Fuels are continuous and homogeneous.

  5. Predicts a surface fire burning in surface fuels.

    1. Does not account for spotting, crowning, or fire whirls.

  6. Weather is uniform and consistent.

  7. Topography is uniform.

Surface/Crown Module

The Surface/Crown module includes additional outputs that are not available in the Surface module. Fire Type is an automated output in this module. The outputs for fire type include surface, torching (passive crown fire), and active. Other outputs, such as rate of spread and flame length, are based on the fire type. Behave 7 uses the same crown fire code as FlamMap, so results in B7 are more consistent with Basic Fire Behavior outputs in FlamMap, IFTDSS, and WFDSS NextGen.

For a surface fire to transition to some type of crown fire, the predicted flame length must be greater than the critical surface flame length.

  • The Transition Ratio output can be used to evaluate surface fire versus some type of crown fire.

The rate of spread must be greater than critical crown rate of spread for a fire to be maintained in the tree canopy as an active crown fire.

  • The Active Ratio output can be used to evaluate torching versus active crown fire.

    FIRE TYPE Active Ratio
    < 1 ≥ 1
    Transition Ratio < 1 Surface Surface
    ≥ 1 Torching (Passive Crown) Active Crown

There are two spotting models available in Surface/Crown.

  • The maximum spotting distance from torching trees model is only available in this module.

  • The maximum spotting distance from an active crown fire model is available in Behave and is based on research from Albini (refer to Albini et al. 2012 and Alexander and Cruz 2014). This spotting model is not available in FlamMap, IFTDSS, or WFDSS NextGen.

Crown Fire Assumptions and Limitations

  1. The model is based on eight spread observations for 7 active crown fires in the western U.S., mostly in the Northern Rockies. These fires burned under unstable conditions; therefore, crown fire calculations do not consider the stability of the atmosphere.

  2. Assumes a wind-driven crown fire.

  3. The model assumes that weather conditions do not change.

  4. The crown fire model is not influenced by slope or aspect. Changes in slope only affect surface fire calculations that drive transition to a crown fire.

  5. Does not predict column collapse.

Surface/Contain Module

This module adds the containment model to the Surface module. The containment model allows users to game out the resource complement needed to contain a spot fire. The spot fire can be a spot fire adjacent to a prescribed fire unit or an initial attack fire.

Assumptions of the Containment Model

  1. Fire is elliptically shaped.

  2. The fuel types do not change along the fire perimeter.

  3. Each resource constructs line equally along both flanks of the fire.

  4. Each resource will have the same production rate for the entire duration of the shift.

Most users will benefit by first calculating the total line production rate needed to contain the fire. While a future version of Behave will hopefully allow this approach, users can currently calculate this by selecting a broad range of inputs for the resource line production rate. Surface/Mortality Module

This module provides the predicted probability of tree mortality based on fire behavior outputs from the Surface module. Due to this module interaction, the intent is to evaluate the thresholds for tree mortality based on fire behavior, such as flame length. Tree mortality is a common objective in prescribed fire plans.

Crown scorch and bark char are the two groups of mortality equations used in the Surface/Mortality module. The mortality equation used depends on the selected tree species. Behave automatically selects the equation in the Surface/Mortality module based on the best available science and displays this information in the Run Results.

  • Crown Scorch – Each species is assigned a mortality model and a bark thickness equation.

  • Bark Char (also referred to as bole char in other publications) – Equations are specific to eastern hardwood species.

Refer to the Behave Documentation, Tips, and History page on FRAMES for additional documentation about tree mortality in the Key Documents.
